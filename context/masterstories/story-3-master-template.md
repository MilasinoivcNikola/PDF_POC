> **Product:** Celebration-of-life booklet — printable memorial program or personal scrapbook
> **Length:** 8 pages
> **Purpose:** A structured, photo-inclusive memorial document that works as either a pet funeral/gathering program OR a personal keepsake scrapbook
> **Status:** Working v1 draft — to be refined by a copy editor before launch
> **Last updated:** June 6, 2026

## How this product is different from Stories 1 and 2

| Dimension | Story 1 | Story 2 | Story 3 |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|
| Voice | Third-person narrator | First-person (pet) | Curatorial / biographical |
| Audience | Child | Adult owner | The family + memorial guests |
| Form | Illustrated picture book | Typeset letter | Photo + text booklet |
| Customer effort | Low (a few inputs) | Medium (quirks + memory) | **High** (timeline, photos, memories) |
| Buyer intent | Tool to help a child | Emotional release | Document a life / share at memorial |

Story 3 is the most **content-heavy** product in the catalog. The customer provides substantially more input than for Stories 1 or 2, including up to 6 photos. The master text below is what wraps and connects their content — the connective tissue, the section headers, the opening and closing passages, and the fallback content when their input is sparse.

This is also the only product designed to be **printed and distributed**, not just framed. Pet memorial gatherings are increasingly common in the US, and families want something physical to hand to attendees. Most customers will print 5-30 copies at FedEx, Staples, or a local print shop.

---

## Dual use case — both must work

Every order needs to function in two contexts:

1. **Memorial program** — handed out at a pet's celebration of life. Guests may not have known the pet well. The booklet must introduce the pet and walk the reader through the family's experience of them.
2. **Personal scrapbook keepsake** — kept by the owner, possibly added to in pen later. May never be shown to anyone else.

The design works for both because the structure is the same. The customer chooses at checkout how many copies to print, but the file is identical either way.

## How to use this document

The master template provides:
- Pre-written opening and closing passages (used verbatim)
- Section headers and connective text
- Fallback content for sections where customer input is sparse
- Structured placeholders for the customer's text and photos
- Layout and design guidance

Workflow per order:
1. Customer fills in extensive input form at checkout (or via follow-up email for Premium tier)
2. Customer uploads up to 6 photos (Premium tier) or receives PDF with photo placeholders to fill themselves (Basic tier)
3. You typeset the booklet in InDesign or Canva using this master template
4. Deliver PDF + a 1-page "how to print and bind this at FedEx" guide

**Production time per order:** 20-40 minutes for first 50 orders. Drops to 10-15 minutes once you build a strong InDesign master template.

**Hire a copy editor** to review the connective text below before launch. Budget: $100-150. The customer inputs will vary wildly in quality — the master text has to hold everything together when their inputs are weak.

## The voice — read this before writing anything

Story 3's voice is **curatorial and dignified** — like a thoughtful eulogy or a well-edited obituary in a small-town newspaper. Not flowery, not clinical. Warm, specific, plainspoken.

**The narrator's voice should:**
- Sound like a respectful family friend writing things down for posterity
- Lean on the customer's specifics — quote them, frame them, never replace them
- Use warm humor where the customer's tone invites it (especially "worst habits")
- Treat the pet as a real being with a real life, not as an abstraction
- Honor the family's grief without performing it
- Read aloud well at a memorial gathering

**The narrator's voice should NOT:**
- Get sentimental in the connective text — let the customer's content carry the emotion
- Use phrases like "our beloved friend has crossed the bridge"
- Eulogize at the customer (the family doesn't need a stranger explaining their loss to them)
- Use the first person ("our hearts go out to…")
- Be funny on the closing page (the closing is always still)

The benchmark to aim for: the writing of a really good local newspaper obituary for a person who was deeply loved. Specific. Grounded. Earned warmth.

## Page-by-page master text

> Layout below assumes 8.5" × 11" portrait, but a square 8" × 8" version is offered as an alternative for customers who want a more "book"-like feel. Default voice is curatorial third-person. Variants noted per page.

---

### PAGE 1 — Cover

**Layout:** Full-bleed photo of the pet on top half; title and dates on bottom half. Or, for the more minimal design option, a small portrait photo centered with white space around it.

**Default cover wording (customer chooses one):**

**Option A — Simple:**
[PET_NAME]
[DATE_BORN] — [DATE_PASSED]

**Option B — Formal:**
In Loving Memory of [PET_NAME]
[DATE_BORN] — [DATE_PASSED]

**Option C — Warm:**
A Life Well Lived
[PET_NAME]
[DATE_BORN] — [DATE_PASSED]

**Option D — Family:**
[PET_NAME]
Beloved [SPECIES_DESCRIPTOR] of [FAMILY_NAME]
[DATE_BORN] — [DATE_PASSED]

*Photo placeholder:* `[PHOTO_COVER]` — primary portrait. Customer's best photo of the pet, alive and themselves.

*Design note:* The cover sets the tone. Customer chooses minimal vs. photo-led at checkout. Recommend minimal for memorial service distribution (more dignified), photo-led for personal keepsake (more intimate).

---

### PAGE 2 — Opening / Dedication

**Pre-written opening passage:**

This is the story of [PET_NAME].

[PET_NAME] was a [BREED], known to family and friends as [PET_NICKNAMES], who joined our lives in [YEAR_ADOPTED] and stayed for [AGE_AT_DEATH] of the best years we have known.

This is not all of [PET_NAME]'s story — no book could hold all of it — but it is the part we remember most, and the part we want to keep.

*Optional [CLOSING_MESSAGE] used here instead if customer wants their own dedication:*

The customer's text appears, italicized, centered:
*"[CLOSING_MESSAGE]"*

— [OWNER_NAMES]

*Illustration / photo placeholder:* Small decorative element (single paw print, leaf, or watercolor wash). No large photo on this page — the cover photo is the introduction; this page is reflective.

*Variant for [PET_NICKNAMES] = blank:* Remove the "known to family and friends as [PET_NICKNAMES]" clause entirely. Keep the sentence clean.

*Variant for [DATE_ADOPTED] = blank or "born into family":* "...who came into our lives in [DATE_BORN] and stayed for [AGE_AT_DEATH] of the best years we have known."

---

### PAGE 3 — Who [PET_NAME] Was

**Layout:** Two-column "biographical card." Left column: photo. Right column: structured facts.

**Right column content (formatted as a list, generous line spacing):**

**Name:** [PET_NAME]
*([PET_NICKNAMES], if provided)*

**Born:** [DATE_BORN]

**Joined our family:** [DATE_ADOPTED]

**Breed:** [BREED]

**Personality:** [PERSONALITY_TRAITS]

**Favorite foods:** [FAVORITE_FOODS]

**Favorite places:** [FAVORITE_PLACES]

**Favorite humans:** [FAVORITE_HUMANS]

**Favorite activities:** [FAVORITE_ACTIVITIES]

**Best qualities:** [BEST_QUALITIES]

**Worst habits:** [WORST_HABITS]

*Photo placeholder:* `[PHOTO_ORIGIN]` — ideally a portrait that shows the pet's face clearly, not action.

*Design note:* This page is the most "yearbook"-feeling page. It's where memorial-service guests will find quick orientation to who this animal was. Pair it with a strong portrait. Keep the labels in a slightly heavier weight so the page scans easily.

*Fallback handling:* If any field is blank, omit that line entirely rather than printing "Favorite foods: —". The page should never feel half-finished.

---

### PAGE 4 — How [PET_NAME] Found Us

**Pre-written opening:**

Every story has a beginning. [PET_NAME]'s began in [DATE_ADOPTED].

[ORIGIN_STORY]

**Pre-written closing of section:**

From that day forward, our family was different. *(Optional second line based on tone of origin story — warmer: "Better. Louder. Hairier. Ours.")*

*Photo placeholder:* `[PHOTO_ORIGIN]` — first day photo, shelter photo, or earliest photo the family has. Often grainy or low-res; that's part of its charm.

*Fallback for [ORIGIN_STORY] = blank or thin:* "[PET_NAME] came to us in [DATE_ADOPTED], and almost immediately, it was as if [PRONOUN_SUBJECT] had always been there. Some animals slot into a family that easily. [PET_NAME] was one of them."

*Variant for [SPECIES] = cat:* The "Better. Louder. Hairier. Ours." closing line works for dogs and rabbits. For cats: "Quieter, in some ways. Louder, in others. Ours."

*Variant for rescue/shelter origin:* If [ORIGIN_STORY] mentions a shelter or rescue, the master text adds a small line at the end: "Whoever loved [PET_NAME] before us, thank you. We took it from there."

---

### PAGE 5 — A Life Together

**Layout:** Vertical timeline with year markers down the left side; events to the right. Up to 2 small photos can be inset along the timeline.

**Page header (pre-written):**

The years went fast — they always do — but here are the ones we want to remember.

**Timeline content (rendered from `[TIMELINE_EVENTS]` input):**

[YEAR_1] — [EVENT_1]
[YEAR_2] — [EVENT_2]
[YEAR_3] — [EVENT_3]
*(up to 6 entries)*

**Pre-written closing line of section:**

A whole life. A good one.

*Photo placeholders:* `[PHOTO_TIMELINE_1]` and `[PHOTO_TIMELINE_2]` — interspersed along the timeline if provided.

*Fallback for thin timeline input:* If the customer provides fewer than 3 events, restructure the page as three larger life-phase blocks:
- "The early years: [BRIEF_NOTE if provided]"
- "The good middle: [BRIEF_NOTE if provided]"
- "The final stretch: [BRIEF_NOTE if provided]"

*Variant for [AGE_AT_DEATH] very young (under 2):* Replace the "The years went fast — they always do" opener with "Not enough years. Not nearly enough. But what we had, we will hold." Then use whatever events the customer provides.

---

### PAGE 6 — Moments to Remember

**Page header (pre-written):**

These are the stories we will tell about [PET_NAME], at dinner tables and family reunions, for the rest of our lives.

**Memory blocks** (rendered from `[FAVORITE_MEMORIES]` input, up to 3):

**[MEMORY_TITLE_1]**
[MEMORY_1]

**[MEMORY_TITLE_2]**
[MEMORY_2]

**[MEMORY_TITLE_3]**
[MEMORY_3]

*Photo placeholder:* `[PHOTO_MEMORY]` — one image that captures the pet at their most themselves. Often customers will pick a slightly silly photo here. That's right.

*If customer doesn't provide titles, generate them from the first few words of the memory.* Or use stock titles: "The Time…", "What [PET_NAME] Did Every Morning", "How [PET_NAME] Said Hello".

*Fallback for [FAVORITE_MEMORIES] = blank:* This is the hardest fallback case in the catalog because the page is structurally about specifics. If a customer leaves this blank, do NOT use generic content. Instead, replace the page with a single block:

"Some of the best things about [PET_NAME] don't fit in stories. They were the daily ones — the way [PRONOUN_SUBJECT] greeted the morning, the sound of nails on the kitchen floor, the small weight of [PRONOUN_OBJECT] at the foot of the bed. The whole life was the memory.

*Space below for family to write or print their own memories at any time.*"

Then provide ruled lines for handwritten addition.

---

### PAGE 7 — What [PET_NAME] Gave Us

**Pre-written opening passage:**

Pets do not give us lessons the way teachers do. They give them the way the weather does — by simply being there, day after day, until we cannot help but learn.

Here is what [PET_NAME] gave us:

**Content (rendered from `[WHAT_THEY_TAUGHT_US]` input):**

If customer provides 3 short lines, render as:
- [LINE_1]
- [LINE_2]
- [LINE_3]

If customer provides longer prose, render as one continuous paragraph.

**Stock fallback content if [WHAT_THEY_TAUGHT_US] is blank:**

- That joy doesn't need a reason.
- That love can be loud or quiet, but it is always there.
- That the small rituals — the morning walk, the spot on the couch, the wait by the door — are the ones we remember.

*Pre-written closing of section:*

We were not the same family before [PET_NAME]. We will not be the same after. That is what it means to have loved [PRONOUN_OBJECT].

*Illustration:* No photo on this page. Generous white space. The text needs room to breathe — this is the most quotable page in the booklet.

---

### PAGE 8 — Closing / In Memoriam

**Pre-written closing passage:**

[PET_NAME] is no longer with us.

But the warmth of [PRONOUN_POSSESSIVE] life — the sounds, the routines, the small weight of [PRONOUN_OBJECT] in our home — is woven into our days, and our memory.

We do not say goodbye.

We say thank you.

**Then, depending on [BELIEF_FRAME]:**

*Rainbow-bridge / heaven variant:* "Rest well, [PET_NAME]. Run easy. We'll see you again."

*Secular variant:* "Rest well, [PET_NAME]. You are kept."

**Followed by:**

With love,
[OWNER_NAMES] *(or [FAMILY_NAME] if provided)*

*Optional [CLOSING_MESSAGE] from family, italicized, below the signature.*

*Photo placeholder:* `[PHOTO_CLOSING]` — final or favorite portrait of the pet. Often a sunset photo, a sleeping photo, or a peaceful image.

**Optional bottom section — only for [SERVICE_TYPE] = memorial_service:**

A ruled "Memorial register" block where attendees can sign their names if the booklet is being passed around at a gathering. 6-8 ruled lines. Header: "Signed by those who loved [PET_NAME]:"

This block is omitted entirely for [SERVICE_TYPE] = personal_keepsake.

## Variants quick reference

### Service type
- **personal_keepsake** (default) — no memorial register on Page 8; tone is intimate
- **memorial_service** — includes signature register on Page 8; tone slightly more formal

### Belief frame
- **rainbow-bridge / heaven** — "Run easy. We'll see you again."
- **secular** — "You are kept."

### Age at death
- **Young pet (under 2)** — adjust Page 5 opener to acknowledge brevity
- **Senior pet (over 10)** — default master text works
- **Very long-lived (15+)** — consider adding "[AGE_AT_DEATH] is a good run for any [SPECIES]. [PET_NAME] did it with style." to Page 5 closing

### Species voice tweaks (subtle, mostly affect Page 4 closing line)
- dog / cat / rabbit / bird / horse / small mammal / reptile / other

## Quality bar / what to avoid

**Never:**
- Quote scripture, songs, or famous poems (copyright + cliché)
- Use stock phrases: "forever in our hearts," "across the bridge," "fur baby," "now an angel"
- Make the narrator emotionally present ("our hearts ache for…") — the narrator is curatorial, not grieving
- Fill blank customer inputs with generic content that pretends specificity
- Use the worst-habits section for actually negative things (the customer wrote it as warm humor; don't sanitize, but don't escalate either)
- Treat the booklet as a vehicle for the narrator's opinions about pet loss
- Add page numbers (this is a keepsake, not a report)

**Always:**
- Let the customer's voice dominate — their words, their humor, their specifics
- Use plenty of white space
- Match photo placement to the emotional weight of each page
- Honor whatever the customer says about their pet, even if it seems odd or specific
- Render the booklet as if it will be reread for years — because it will

## Typography & layout guide

**Recommended typeface stack:**
- Body: Lora (Google), 11-12pt, 1.4 line spacing
- Headers: Cormorant Garamond, bold or semibold, 18-24pt
- Captions / dates: Lora italic, 9-10pt

**Page setup:**
- 8.5" × 11" portrait (default) or 8" × 8" square (alternative)
- 0.75" margins (allows for FedEx booklet binding)
- Page numbers: **none**
- Header/footer: none

**Color:**
- Black ink on cream or warm white (never pure white)
- Photo prints in color, but a "monochrome / sepia" option should be offered for customers who want a more vintage feel
- A single accent color used sparingly for headers (suggest: dusty rose, sage, slate, dusty navy)

**Photo specifications:**
- Customer uploads must be at least 1500px on long edge for full-page placement, 800px for inline
- Auto-warn customers at upload if photo is too small
- Premium tier: light photo retouching included (basic crop, color correction, no Photoshop magic)

**Binding recommendations to include in customer's print-guide PDF:**
- FedEx Office "Saddle Stitch Booklet" — $1.50-3 per booklet for 8 pages, color print
- Staples Print & Marketing — similar pricing
- Home printing + simple staple binding for budget
- Spiral binding for keepsake durability

## Notes for the ghostwriter / reviewer

This draft is the starting point. Areas where I'd most welcome edits:

1. **Page 2 opening passage** — "no book could hold all of it" might be too sentimental, or it might be exactly right. Read aloud and judge.
2. **Page 7 (What They Gave Us)** — the stock fallback lines ("joy doesn't need a reason," etc.) need to be strong enough to carry the page on their own when customers leave it blank. Are they?
3. **Page 8 closing variants** — "Run easy. We'll see you again." vs. "You are kept." Both have to land. The secular one is harder.
4. **Worst Habits section on Page 3** — how do we make sure customers feel permission to be warmly funny here? Consider adding a tooltip at checkout: "This is where the love shows. Don't sanitize."
5. **Fallback for blank `[FAVORITE_MEMORIES]`** — currently I replace the page with a meditation + ruled lines for handwritten addition. Is this the right call, or should we prompt the customer harder before delivery?
6. **The age-at-death variant for very young pets** — is the brevity-acknowledging line on Page 5 ("Not enough years. Not nearly enough.") too much? Or right?
7. **Memorial register on Page 8** — should we include this even for personal_keepsake orders, just smaller? Some customers may want guests at a future gathering to sign.

**Total ghostwriter + reviewer budget guidance:** $150-250. Story 3 has lower stakes per individual sentence than Story 2, but more sentences. The editor's job is consistency and connective tissue more than poetry.
