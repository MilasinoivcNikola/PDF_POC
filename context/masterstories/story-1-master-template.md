# Story 1 Master Template — "Saying Goodbye to [PET_NAME]"

> **Product:** Hero product — personalized PDF storybook for children ages 4-12
> **Length:** 12 pages + cover + dedication
> **Purpose:** Help a parent gently explain pet loss to a child
> **Status:** Working v1 draft — to be refined by a children's grief specialist before launch
> **Last updated:** June 6, 2026

---

## How to use this document

This is the master text. Every paid order generates a PDF by substituting the merge fields below into the page-by-page text on this template, then matching it with the correct age-bracket variant and illustration set.

For launch, plan to manually produce each PDF in InDesign or Canva using this template as the source of truth. Automate only once you've sold 100+ books.

**Hire a children's grief specialist or counselor to review this final** before going live. The single biggest quality bar in this niche is whether the text reads as written by someone who understands child grief. Budget $100-150 for review on Upwork or Fiverr.

## Story arc (why this structure works)

The arc is built on established children's grief literature principles:

1. **Establish the bond** before death is mentioned (Pages 3-6) — the child must feel the pet is alive in the story before processing the loss.
2. **Use the word "died"** — not "passed away," "went to sleep," or "lost." Euphemisms confuse young children and can create new fears (e.g., fear of sleep).
3. **Validate every feeling** without prescribing how the child should feel.
4. **Offer a comfort frame** (Rainbow Bridge / heaven / "still in our memory") matched to the family's beliefs.
5. **Give the child something to do** — ritual reduces helplessness.
6. **End with love that persists**, not with finality.

The story should take 4-6 minutes to read aloud. Designed to be read more than once.

## Page-by-page master text

> **Notation:** Text in regular type is the published wording. *Italic notes* are for the ghostwriter/designer. Page numbers refer to the printed/PDF layout. Default tone below is calibrated for **age 6-8**. Variants for 3-5 and 9-12 are in the next section.

---

### COVER

**Title (large, hand-lettered feel):**
Saying Goodbye to [PET_NAME]

**Subtitle (smaller):**
A story for [CHILD_NAME]

*Illustration brief:* [PET_NAME] (a [BREED_COLOR] [SPECIES]) sitting in warm afternoon light with [CHILD_NAME] (a child, age-appropriate, gender-neutral if not specified) gently leaning against [PRONOUN_OBJECT]. Soft sunset palette. The pet should look gentle, not sad. This is the "we loved each other" image, not the "goodbye" image.

---

### PAGE 1 — Dedication

For [CHILD_NAME],
and for [PET_NAME],
who loved [PRONOUN_OBJECT] so very much.

*Optional:* [PARENT_DEDICATION] *(if parent provided one, printed below in a different typeface, smaller)*

*Illustration brief:* Single portrait of [PET_NAME] alone, looking softly toward the reader. Soft pastel border (watercolor wash). The pet should look the way the customer remembers them — alive, present, themselves.

---

### PAGE 2 — Opening

Once, in a home full of love, there lived a [SPECIES] named [PET_NAME].

[PET_NAME] was a [BREED_COLOR].

And [PRONOUN_SUBJECT] always knew, somehow, when you needed a friend.

*Illustration brief:* [PET_NAME] at the front door of a cozy home, looking up as if greeting the reader. Warm interior lighting visible through window. Body language: alert, happy, tail position friendly.

---

### PAGE 3 — The Bond

[PET_NAME] loved [CHILD_NAME] more than anything in the whole world.

And [CHILD_NAME] loved [PET_NAME] right back.

*Illustration brief:* [PET_NAME] and [CHILD_NAME] together in a quiet moment — perhaps the child reading to the pet, or the pet's head resting on the child's lap. Eye contact between them. This is the emotional anchor of the book.

---

### PAGE 4 — Daily Joys

Every day was an adventure together.

[PET_NAME]'s favorite thing in the world was [FAVORITE_ACTIVITY].

*Illustration brief:* [PET_NAME] doing [FAVORITE_ACTIVITY], full of energy and life. Bright, joyful palette here — this is the "peak alive" image. The child may or may not be in this image (better if just the pet, to keep focus).

---

### PAGE 5 — Safe Places

And when the day was done, [PRONOUN_SUBJECT] would curl up [SLEEPING_SPOT], where it was warm and safe.

That was [PET_NAME]'s favorite place to dream.

*Illustration brief:* [PET_NAME] sleeping peacefully [SLEEPING_SPOT]. Soft, low light. Cozy. This image is important — it sets up the "peaceful rest" feeling that will be echoed on Page 7.

---

### PAGE 6 — The Memory

There was one day [CHILD_NAME] will always remember.

[FAVORITE_MEMORY]

That was the kind of love [PET_NAME] and [CHILD_NAME] shared.

*Illustration brief:* This is the customizable page — illustration should evoke the memory the parent shared. If memory is too specific to illustrate, use a warm "looking at each other" image of the child and pet, with golden hour light. *Ghostwriter note: if [FAVORITE_MEMORY] is more than 2 sentences, trim it to its core moment.*

---

### PAGE 7 — The Gentle Truth *(the hard page)*

But every living thing has a beginning, a middle, and an end. Even the ones we love most.

[PET_NAME]'s body got tired.

And then, very gently, [PRONOUN_POSSESSIVE] body stopped working. That is what "died" means.

*Illustration brief:* [PET_NAME] resting peacefully — eyes closed, body relaxed, paws tucked. NOT scary, NOT lifeless. Soft, warm tones. This should mirror the sleeping image on Page 5 — same posture, same comfort — to gently associate death with peaceful rest. **Do not** show the body in a hospital, on a vet table, or anything clinical.

*Variant for [DEATH_TYPE] = euthanasia:* "When a pet's body is too tired or hurting, sometimes a doctor helps them stop hurting. That is the kindest thing we can do for someone we love. [PET_NAME]'s body stopped working, very gently, with people who loved [PRONOUN_OBJECT] right there."

*Variant for [DEATH_TYPE] = sudden:* "Sometimes, [PET_NAME]'s body got tired all at once, without warning. We didn't get to say goodbye the way we wanted to. That is one of the hardest parts. It is not anyone's fault."

---

### PAGE 8 — Feelings

This might make [CHILD_NAME] feel a lot of things.

Sad. Maybe angry. Maybe like nothing feels right anymore.

Maybe like crying. Maybe like not crying at all.

All of these feelings are okay.

They mean [CHILD_NAME] loved [PET_NAME] very, very much.

*Illustration brief:* [CHILD_NAME] sitting quietly, perhaps holding [PET_NAME]'s collar, leash, or favorite toy. Expression: thoughtful, not weeping. The child is processing, not in crisis. An adult figure (parent's shape, no specific face) may be sitting nearby, present but giving space.

---

### PAGE 9 — A Comforting Frame

*Default variant ([BELIEF_FRAME] = rainbow-bridge):*

Some people say there is a place called the Rainbow Bridge.

A sunny meadow where pets run free, where bodies don't get tired anymore.

Where [PET_NAME] is free to spend [PRONOUN_POSSESSIVE] days [FAVORITE_ACTIVITY].

*Variant ([BELIEF_FRAME] = heaven):*

Many families believe that pets who die go to a peaceful place — a sunny meadow, or heaven, or somewhere their bodies are not tired anymore.

Wherever [PET_NAME] is now, [PRONOUN_SUBJECT] is at peace.

*Variant ([BELIEF_FRAME] = secular / none):*

[PET_NAME]'s body is not here anymore.

But the [PET_NAME] that [CHILD_NAME] loved — [PRONOUN_POSSESSIVE] silly bark, [PRONOUN_POSSESSIVE] soft fur, [PRONOUN_POSSESSIVE] favorite spot [SLEEPING_SPOT] — all of that stays in our hearts. That is where [PET_NAME] lives now.

*Illustration brief:* For rainbow-bridge/heaven variants — peaceful meadow at golden hour, [PET_NAME] running freely, soft clouds, gentle light. Not too "fantastical" — keep it earthy, like a real sunlit field. For secular variant — soft glowing image of [PET_NAME] over a heart shape, or [CHILD_NAME] looking at a photo of [PET_NAME] with a glow around the memory.

---

### PAGE 10 — Love Stays

Here is the most important thing of all.

The love between [CHILD_NAME] and [PET_NAME] did not stop when [PET_NAME] died.

Love does not work that way.

It stays. It always stays.

*Illustration brief:* [CHILD_NAME] with hand on heart, [PET_NAME]'s gentle silhouette or warm light beside them. Symbolic, slightly dreamlike. This is the most quoted page in customer reviews — make this illustration the strongest one in the book.

---

### PAGE 11 — Things to Do

When [CHILD_NAME] misses [PET_NAME] — and that will happen many times — there are things [CHILD_NAME] can do.

Look at a picture and remember a happy day.

Tell a story about [PET_NAME] to someone who will listen.

Say [PET_NAME]'s name out loud — names are meant to be said.

And know that, wherever [PET_NAME] is, [PRONOUN_SUBJECT] is loved.

*Variant ([OTHER_PETS_IN_HOME] = yes):* Add a final line — "And when the other pets in your home want extra cuddles, they probably miss [PET_NAME] too. You can comfort each other."

*Illustration brief:* Three small vignettes in a triptych:
1. [CHILD_NAME] looking at a framed photo of [PET_NAME]
2. [CHILD_NAME] telling a story to a parent or sibling
3. [CHILD_NAME] outdoors, looking up at the sky or a meadow, saying [PET_NAME]'s name

---

### PAGE 12 — Closing

[PET_NAME] was a good [SPECIES_DESCRIPTOR].

[PET_NAME] was [CHILD_NAME]'s [SPECIES].

And [PET_NAME] will always, always be loved.

*Illustration brief:* The closing image — [PET_NAME] and [CHILD_NAME] together in their happiest scene, warm golden light, slightly dreamlike to suggest memory. This image should mirror the cover but feel more eternal — perhaps with a soft glow, or seen from slightly farther away.

---

### BACK COVER — Memory Page

*Layout:* Space for parents to write or print their own message. Provided as an editable field in the PDF.

**Suggested prompt printed at top of page:**
"A place to write a memory of [PET_NAME]"

*Optional template lines:*
- The day [PET_NAME] came home: ___________
- [PET_NAME]'s favorite sound: ___________
- The funniest thing [PET_NAME] ever did: ___________
- One thing [PET_NAME] taught me: ___________

*Illustration brief:* Soft border around the writing space — perhaps paw prints, a flower, or a gentle frame. Should not compete with the handwritten content.

## Age-bracket variants

### For [CHILD_AGE_BRACKET] = 3-5 (Preschool)

**Goal:** Maximum simplicity. Short sentences. Concrete language only. No abstract concepts.

**Adjustments:**
- Replace Page 7's "every living thing has a beginning, a middle, and an end" with: "After a long, happy life, [PET_NAME]'s body got very tired. And then [PRONOUN_POSSESSIVE] body stopped working. That is what 'died' means."
- Replace Page 8's full feeling list with: "This might make [CHILD_NAME] feel sad. Maybe very sad. It's okay to feel sad. It means you loved [PET_NAME]."
- Page 11: Reduce to two actions, not four. "Look at a picture and remember. Say [PET_NAME]'s name."
- Keep illustrations larger, text smaller, on every page.

### For [CHILD_AGE_BRACKET] = 6-8 (Default)

The master text above is calibrated for this age.

### For [CHILD_AGE_BRACKET] = 9-12 (Older child)

**Goal:** Treat the child more like the adult-in-training they are. Honor their bigger feelings and questions.

**Adjustments:**
- Page 7: Add a sentence — "It is one of the hardest things about being alive: the people and animals we love don't stay forever. But the love does."
- Page 8: Add — "Some days [CHILD_NAME] will feel okay, and then [PRONOUN_SUBJECT] will feel sad again out of nowhere. Grief is like that. It comes in waves."
- Page 9 (only if [DEATH_TYPE] = euthanasia): "If [CHILD_NAME] is wondering whether anything could have been done differently — the answer is no. Letting [PET_NAME] go was the most loving choice." *(Older kids often blame themselves or their parents; address this directly.)*
- Page 11: Add — "And when [CHILD_NAME] is ready, it is okay to love another pet someday. Loving a new pet does not replace [PET_NAME]. It just means [CHILD_NAME]'s heart is big enough for both."

## Quality bar / what to avoid

**Never:**
- Use "lost," "passed away," "went to sleep," or "went away" instead of "died" (creates new fears)
- Promise the pet "is watching over" the child (creates unfulfillable expectation)
- Suggest the child will "see [PET_NAME] again someday" (this is a parent's decision based on belief)
- Tell the child how to feel ("don't be sad, [PET_NAME] is in a better place")
- End on a sad note — the last full page must be hopeful
- Use words a 6-year-old wouldn't understand without explanation
- Make the pet's death the parent's fault, the vet's fault, or anyone's fault

**Always:**
- Validate that grief is the cost of love
- Use the pet's name often — it humanizes them
- Give the child a small action they can take
- Acknowledge that grief will return in waves, not vanish
- Treat the child as capable of handling the truth, in age-appropriate doses

## Illustration style guide

For visual consistency across the book and across orders:

- **Style options offered to customer:** soft watercolor (default), gentle storybook (cleaner lines, like Oliver Jeffers), pencil sketch (more muted, for older children)
- **Palette:** warm pastels, golden hour light, no harsh contrasts, no pure black
- **Pet consistency:** same breed markings, eye color, body posture across every page — this is the #1 quality issue for AI illustrations. Lock a reference image of the pet before generating any page art.
- **Child rendering:** keep the child's face slightly stylized / 3/4 view or from behind — avoids uncanny-valley problems and lets any child see themselves in the image
- **Avoid:** photographic realism (jarring in a grief context), bright primary colors, anything that looks like clipart

## Notes for the ghostwriter / reviewer

This draft is the starting point. Areas where I'd most welcome a specialist's edit:

1. **Page 7** — the death page. The phrasing of "body stopped working" is grounded in child-grief best practice but could be polished. Verify it reads gently aloud.
2. **Page 8** — the feelings page. Consider whether the list should be longer, shorter, or restructured.
3. **Death-type variant for euthanasia (Page 7)** — extremely sensitive; verify with someone who has counseled families through this.
4. **Page 11** — the actions page. Are these the right four actions? Should one of them involve a physical ritual (drawing, lighting a candle)?
5. **Older-child variant** — particularly the "love another pet someday" line. Some families will love it; others will find it premature. Consider making this an optional toggle.

**Total ghostwriter budget guidance:** $200-400 for a polish + specialist review. Worth every dollar.
