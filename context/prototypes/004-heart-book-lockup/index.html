Dearbound · Iteration 004 · Heart-book lockup

Locking the Open Heart-Book.

Direction 03 is chosen — an open book whose two facing pages curve up into the lobes of a heart. This iteration locks the final glyph, shows the decided two-worlds tint (neutral in the global header, gold on living pages, rose on loss pages), draws a favicon-optimized variant of the same mark at 48 / 32 / 16px, and puts four wordmark treatments in the live nav so you can pick the type.

My one recommendation in this iteration

Lock the wordmark as letter-spaced small-caps — “Dearbound.”

The glyph and tint are decided; the only open call is the type. Small-caps keeps the warmth of Fraunces but reads as a considered imprint / press rather than a default page title — which is exactly the hand-finished keepsake positioning, and it pairs with the heart-book without competing with it. Full rationale and four side-by-side options below.

01 The locked glyph

The hero mark, refined from 003. Two facing pages rise into a heart; the open covers spread at the base; a center spine ties them together. Drawn in ink so it’s the stable, neutral brand mark — the color tint is applied per surface (next section), not baked into the glyph. Shown large beside the in-context wordmark lockup.

02 Two-worlds tint — decided

Neutral ink in the global site header (landing, catalog, anywhere not committed to a world), gold on living / celebration pages, rose on loss / memorial pages — echoing the existing accent system. Only the glyph’s ink strokes and the spine take the tint; the soft page-wash stays --rose-faint so the heart always reads warm. Here are the three states in the live header, side by side.

The tint reads cleanly in all three: ink is authoritative and neutral, gold warms toward celebration, rose softens toward memorial — and because the page-wash stays the same warm rose-faint, the mark never feels like a different logo, just the same mark wearing the page’s mood. Implementation note for the engineer: the glyph would take its stroke from a CSS variable (e.g. --mark-ink) set per route group, defaulting to --ink; the existing gold/rose page accents already exist as tokens.

03 Favicon — the heart-book itself, optimized

Per your call: keep the full heart-book at favicon scale on a transparent background — not a bare heart, not a monogram. In 003 the literal hero mark went mushy at 16px (the thin covers + page-lines collapsed and it read as just a heart). So this is a favicon-tuned redraw of the same mark: the inner page-lines are dropped, the strokes thicken as the size shrinks, and the open covers are simplified to two bold wedges — so it still reads book-becoming-a-heart, not just heart. Honest read of how it holds up. 48px: excellent — full outline mark, covers + spine + heart all legible. 32px: good — the filled-wedge / solid-heart reduction with the knocked-out spine notch keeps the two-part “book + heart” read; this is the size that does the most work (Retina tab @2×). 16px: honest verdict — it reads as a heart sitting on an open book, which is the right idea, but at exactly 16px CSS it is close to its legibility floor: the spine notch is the single feature separating “book + heart” from “a heart on a base,” and on a very dark browser-tab chrome the transparent ink mark can disappear. Two things to confirm with the engineer at build: (a) ship a real multi-size .ico + an SVG favicon so 16/32/48 each use the size-tuned drawing above rather than auto-scaling one file; (b) consider a one-pixel lighter ink or a faint outline for dark-mode tab chrome. Within those caveats the same mark survives — it does not have to fall back to a generic heart.

04 Wordmark — four treatments to pick from

The glyph is locked; the type beside it is the open decision. Each option is shown in the live header next to the locked neutral glyph, plus a larger lockup so you can read the letterforms. One-line rationale each; my pick is marked.

ATitle-case Fraunces (the control — what ships today)

“Dearbound,” exactly as the current wordmark renders it (opsz 144, SOFT 50, title case). Warmest and most book-like, zero migration. The risk: it reads as a word set in a nice serif rather than a designed mark — fine, but it leans on the new glyph to do all the brand-identity work.

Dearbound

BLowercase “dearbound”

“dearbound,” all lowercase. Softer, quieter, more intimate — leans into the “dear” and feels modern-craft (think indie press / stationery). The risk in a grief-adjacent product: lowercase can read slightly casual / startup, undercutting the dignity a memorial title wants; and the unique “D” of “Dearbound” disappears.

CLetter-spaced caps “D E A R B O U N D”

Tracked-out full caps. Most formal and architectural — reads like a fine-press colophon or a foil-stamped spine. Carries dignity for memorial pages effortlessly. The risk: full caps at the larger letterspacing can feel slightly cold / fashion-house for the joyful gotcha-day titles, and it’s the widest lockup (watch the header on narrow screens).

Dearbound

DLetter-spaced small-caps “Dearbound”

The fourth option I chose. Real Fraunces small-caps with a gentle .10em tracking — the cap “D” stays as a quiet initial, the rest sit as even small caps. It’s the middle ground that takes the best of B and C: the imprint/press feel and dignity of caps (D), but warmer and more even-toned, never cold, so it carries celebration and memorial. Most “considered mark, not default title,” and it sits beautifully under the heart-book’s symmetry. My recommendation.
