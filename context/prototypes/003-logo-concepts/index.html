Dearbound · Iteration 003 · Logo concepts

A mark for Dearbound.

Five directions for replacing the plain text wordmark in the site header. Each is shown as a hero lockup, in the live nav context, on light + gold (living) + rose (loss) surfaces, and at favicon scale. All built from the existing system: Fraunces display type, the real palette tokens, the gold = celebration / rose = memorial accent split.

Recommendation

Lead with Direction 02 — “The Bound Spine.”

It’s the only mark that says the actual product — a bound book — and quietly encodes the name (“-bound”) without a single literal pet cliché. The book-spine glyph reads as warm and editorial at hero scale, survives down to a 16px favicon as a clean stacked-pages mark, and is emotionally neutral, so it carries a joyful gotcha-day title and a memorial letter equally well. Direction 04 (“Tucked Paw”) is the close runner-up if you want the pet present in the mark itself.

01 Refined Paw — the system today, elevated

What it says: the gentlest evolution of what ships now — the existing paw-print ornament, redrawn as a single soft, hand-inked shape rather than the current five disconnected blobs, and tucked tight against the wordmark. Why it fits: lowest-risk, instantly “Dearbound,” and the paw is the universal pet signifier — but it leans slightly cute and says “pet brand” more than “keepsake book.” Best if you want continuity over a statement.

02 The Bound Spine — a book, and the name

What it says: a small stack of bound pages seen edge-on — a book spine with three sewn signatures and a thread stitch through them. It names the product (a bound book) and puns the “-bound” in Dearbound, without any literal animal. Why it fits: emotionally neutral, so it holds a celebration and a memorial title with the same dignity; reads as “keepsake / press / bindery,” which is exactly the hand-finished positioning. The stitch is the warm, made-by-hand detail. Recommended.

At 16px the stitch and outer pages collapse to a clean 2-line stacked-book glyph — still legibly “a bound book.”

03 The Open Heart-Book — a book that is also a heart

What it says: an open book whose two facing pages curve up into the lobes of a heart. Love + the book, in one form. Why it fits: the single most emotionally honest mark here — it’s about love, which is the through-line of every title, joyful or grieving. The trade-off: the heart pulls it toward sentiment; on a memorial title it’s tender, on a gotcha-day title it’s sweet, but it has the least “editorial restraint” of the five. Strong if you want warmth foregrounded over craft.

04 Tucked Paw — a paw inside a page

What it says: a single dog-eared page (the corner folded, like a bookmark in a story) with a small paw pressed gently into it — the pet, kept inside the book. It marries the two ideas the first three split apart. Why it fits: says “pet” and “book / keepsake” in one quiet form, and the folded corner is a warm, human detail (we mark the pages that matter). Carries both worlds well; reads as tender without tipping cute. Runner-up to 02.

05 The Monogram — a typographic “D” seal

What it says: no picture at all — a Fraunces “D” drawn as a bound book (the straight stem is the spine, the bowl is the cover), set in a hand-pressed circular seal like a colophon or a wax stamp on the last page of a keepsake. Why it fits: the most grown-up, press-like and timeless option; fully neutral across both worlds; scales to a favicon better than any pictorial mark because it’s essentially one letter. The trade-off: the least warm and the least “pet” — it asks the wordmark and the books to carry all the personality.
